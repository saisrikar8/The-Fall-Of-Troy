THE FALL OF TROY

Troy

Troy was an ancient city in northwestern Anatolia and holds an enduring place in both literature and archaeology. It also had lied on the trade routes between Europe and Asia. The legend of the Trojan War fought between the Greeks and the people of Troy is the most notable theme from ancient Greek literature and forms the basis of Homer's Iliad. The ruins of Troy are a key archeological site that shos the development of civilization in northwestern Anatolia. The ruins of Troy wer enrolled as a UNESCO World Heritage site in 1998. Troy controlled a strategic point at the southern entrance to the Dardanelles Strait, which linked the Black Sea with the Aegean Sea. The city also controlled a land route that went north along the west Anatolian coast and crossed the narrowest point of the Dardanelles to the European shore.

The Trojan War

As stated before, the story of the Trojan War fought between the Greeks and the people of Troy is one of the most widely known themes in Homer's epics. It started when Aphrodite, goddess of love, promised Paris the most beautiful mortal woman in the world. At the time, the most beautiful woman was Helen, queen of Sparta and wife of King Menelaus. When Paris went to visit Menelaus, he kidnapped Helen and took her to Troy. King Menelaus was infuriated and recruited warriors from all over Greece in order to get his wife back and attack Troy. He recruited warriars from all over Greece. One of these warriors was a master strategist named Odysseus. For ten years, there was a war between the Greeks and the Trojans called the Trojan War, but the Greeks were never able to get past the walls of Troy. This is where Odysseus became important. He thought of a plan that could get into the city, which began the TROJAN WAR.
